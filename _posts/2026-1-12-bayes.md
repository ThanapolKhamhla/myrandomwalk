Suppose a table is hidden from our view and a white ball is thrown and rolled on the table. By doing so, the thrower has already ensured his throw is not biased and completely random. When the ball stops, the vertical line is drawn across the table and passes where the ball is, the ball is then removed. 

![bayes1]({{ "/assets/images/2025-08-18-bayes_1.pdf" | relative_url }})


Since the table is hidden from us, the position of a line is also unknown to us. Then, Some red balls are thrown at random onto the same table. When all red balls come to rest, we are told only how many lie to the left and how many to the right of the line. Where should we put a guess of position of the line? And if another red ball is thrown, what should be the probability of the ball lying to the left of the line?”

Let say there was 5 red balls thrown alfter the white ball. and we are told that there are 3 red balls on the left on the line. What is the best guess for the first question? In first glance I answer 2/5 of the table right away. Since the only information i have is that there are 2 balls on the left so I thought that the line should locate at number of red ball on the left divided by total number of red balls

Bayes said that I should put a guess at 3/7 of the way up from the left and i arrived at my wrong conclusion because I forgot to take ‘prior probability’ from the first white ball into account. To arraive to the same answer, I have to start with prior probability that is the probability of the white ball to land on some particular point. And I must add new information, probability of red balls to land on some particular point, to the information in my hand to obtain ‘posterior probability. So i cannot say just that ‘red balls should spred evenly across the table’ but should say that ‘they spred evenly after the line was drawn somewhere’. The line could be anywhere like in the case of red balls. I am compltetely blind to where the line is, then I could not bias toward either side. So the average position should be in the middle. It might sound like useless information, but at least I have some piece to start with. Bayes said that the real equation to estimate most likely place of the line is number of red ball plus one divided by total number of red ball plus two. Where in the world plus one and plus two came from? It sounds like 2 imaginary balls are added to our 5 red balls  before they are thown at the table.

# Bayes prevent me from being a fortune teller 

Let us define some notation so we can reduce the words to discuss the problem. First, we'll denote the position of the line by $p$. How can we guess where it is, or to say what is the most likely place of $p$? In the first place, the line could be equlally likely anywhere . That is our first piece of information. If we assume that the table has a length of 1, so $p$ can be written as 

$$
p = uniform(0,1)
$$

Given a hypothetical value of $p$ and the observed number y of red balls on the left of the line, and suppose that total number of red ball is $n$,  we can compute the likelihood of that event,$l$, in form of 

$$
l = binomial(n,y,p) = (p)^y(1-p)^(n-y)
$$

if we leave our prior probability behind and use only this new information, we would calculate the maximum likelihood estimate for $p$ by simply differentiate $l$ to find a local maximum at

$$
l = y/n
$$

which is the proportion of red balls that ended up to the left of the line ans arrive at the answer of 2/5 I got in first place. To see how this estimation is wrong, we must get back to the second question: if another red ball is thrown, what should be the probability of the ball lying to the left of the line? by this way, we could say that the next ball would lie on the left of the line with probability of 2/5, nothing wired, may be. However, if somehow all 5 red balls happened to fall on the left of the line, the probability of the next red ball to lie on the left of the line will be 5/5 or 1. So I can tell that it will certainly fall on the left of the line no matter how many additional red ball are thrown, can I? Bayes said that even though all 5 balls fall on the left, the probability of the next ball to fall on the left is only 6/7. So he stole the rest probability of 1/7 from my prediction. i cannot be one of those fortune tellers, who might live in some corner of this univesre (i cannot say with complete certainty that fortune teller do not exist) so I obviously cannot say for sure that the ball will be on the left.

To get to the right answer accoding to bayes ,we have to integrate this l into our prior probability of uniform distribution, $p$, But how? Fortunately, our prior can be expressed in the form of beta distribution

$$
(p)^a(1-p)^b
$$

![bayes2]({{ "/assets/images/2025-08-18-bayes_2.pdf" | relative_url }})

This grapgh show beta ditrubtion from different parameter $a$ and $b$. It turn out that our uniform distribution is just one apecial case of this distribution where $a = b = 1$. By this way, we can work with case where we might have other information that suggests $p$ isn’t uniformly distributed on [0, 1] but has some other distribution. Given prior and new information, our posterior probability, $l'$ will be preportional to product of pior and new information

$$
l' = (p)^y+1(1-p)^n-y+1
$$

Given this posterior probability distribution, we can, again, differentiate it to find the maximum likelihood estimate for $p$. The result is 

$$
y+1/n+2
$$

That where plus one and plus two came from. With this probability, even though 1000 red balls fall on the left of the line, I can only expect the next ball to also fall on the left with probability of 1001/1002 = 0.999002. Everything makes more sense now.

# Let’s throw real balls

To see that what we have discuss so far is practically correct, let's do some simulation. 

```
def Bayesian_table(n_trials = 1000000, n_red_ball = 5):
    # throw 1 white ball onto n_trials tables
    white_ball = np.random.uniform(low=0, high=1, size=n_trials) 
    # throw n_red_ball red ball onto n_trials tables
    red_balls = np.random.uniform(low=0, high=1, size = (n_red_ball, n_trials)) 
    # count the number of red balls to the left of the white ball on each trial
    n_red_ball_left = np.sum(red_balls < white_ball, axis=0) 
    # record position of white ball if exacly 2 red ball lie on the left of the line
    white_ball_pos = white_ball[n_red_ball_left == 2] 
    
    return white_ball_pos
```

we can plot the result with $p$ or the position of the line on the x-axis and frequency or how many times the line is drawn at that point in y-axis. The result is, as expected, followed posterior beta distribution

When we want to address the second question, What is the probability of the next red ball falling on the left side of the line if all 5 red balls fall on the left, we can extend from the same code in which I record only position of the line in the trials that exactly 5 red balls land on the left. Then, ‘next_red_ball’ is thrown and stop somewhere randomly with uniform distribution. Therefore, the probability will be The number of trial that next_red_ball fall on the left divided by total nuber of trials, which comes out as 0.8576, very close to 6/7. 

```
def Bayesian_table(n_trials = 1000000, n_red_ball = 5):
    # throw 1 white ball onto n_trials tables
    white_ball = np.random.uniform(low=0, high=1, size=n_trials) 
    # throw n_red_ball red ball onto n_trials tables
    red_balls = np.random.uniform(low=0, high=1, size = (n_red_ball, n_trials)) 
    # count the number of red balls to the left of the white ball on each trial
    n_red_ball_left = np.sum(red_balls < white_ball, axis=0) 
    # record position of white ball if exacly 2 red ball lie on the left of the line
    white_ball_pos = white_ball[n_red_ball_left == 5] 
    
    next_red_ball = np.random.uniform(low=0, high=1, size=len(white_ball_pos)) 
    next_red_ball_prob = np.sum(next_red_ball < white_ball_pos, axis=0) / len(white_ball_pos)
     
    return next_red_ball_prob
```
